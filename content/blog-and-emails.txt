What Is the Nine-Tailed Fox? A Complete Guide to the Jiuweihu (九尾狐)

She has graced the pages of China's oldest myths, the screens of its most-watched dramas, and the imagination of an entire civilization for over two millennia. She is the nine-tailed fox — the Jiuweihu — and her story is far more layered than any single legend can contain.

The Earliest Sightings

The first written record of the nine-tailed fox appears in the Shanhaijing (Classic of Mountains and Seas), a text compiled between the 4th and 1st centuries BCE. On Green-Hills Mountain, the book tells us, there lives a beast "shaped like a fox with nine tails. It makes a sound like a crying infant." In some passages she is a man-eater. In others, she is an auspicious omen — a sign of peace and prosperity that appears only when a virtuous ruler sits on the throne.

Yu the Great, the mythical founder of the Xia dynasty, encountered a white nine-tailed fox and interpreted it as a sign that he would marry the woman he loved. The fox, in this telling, was a witness to destiny — a creature woven into the fabric of fate itself.

From Celestial Fox to Seductress

In early China, the nine-tailed fox was revered. The scholar Guo Pu (276–324 CE) wrote that a fox who lives a thousand years transforms into a celestial fox — golden-furred, nine-tailed, serving in the Palace of the Sun and Moon. She transcends yin and yang. She is a creature of power, not of trickery.

But as dynasties turned, so did her reputation. By the Song dynasty, the nine-tailed fox had been recast as a seductive soul-stealer. The most famous version is Daji, the fox spirit who possessed a general's daughter and brought down the Shang dynasty through cruelty and manipulation. In this telling, she is a cautionary figure — beauty as both gift and warning.

Today, both versions coexist. She is the benevolent matchmaker. She is the dangerous enchantress. She is nine lives woven into one form.

The Nine Tails

Why nine tails? In Chinese numerology, nine (九, jiu) is the number of the emperor — the pinnacle of yang energy, completion, and heaven. Each tail is said to represent a different aspect of power: wisdom, beauty, wealth, love, protection, transformation, foresight, influence, and immortality. To wear the nine-tailed fox is to carry all nine energies with you.

The Modern Muse

From the anime Naruto to the drama Eternal Love, from K-dramas to Hollywood, the nine-tailed fox has crossed every border. She is the most exported creature in Chinese mythology — instantly recognizable, endlessly reinterpreted.

Which Chinese Guardian Matches Your Personality?

In Chinese cosmology, the world is watched over by four celestial guardians — the Four Symbols (四象, Si Xiang). Each commands a direction, a season, and a constellation of seven lunar mansions. Each embodies a different temperament, a different kind of soul.

One of them is watching over you right now. The question is: which one?

Azure Dragon (Qinglong) — The Visionary

Direction: East | Season: Spring | Element: Wood

You wake up early. You see possibilities where others see obstacles. The Azure Dragon is the guardian of spring — of what begins, what unfurls, what dares. If you are the person who starts the company, plans the trip, sends the first message, then Qinglong recognizes you as kin.

The Dragon type is ambitious but quiet about it. You build steadily, like wood growing toward light. You lead not by commanding but by embodying. Your presence, like spring itself, makes things grow.

Personality at a glance: Ambitious, visionary, calm, quietly powerful.
Your season: Spring. Your direction: East — where the sun rises on everything yet to be built.

White Tiger (Baihu) — The Protector

Direction: West | Season: Autumn | Element: Metal

You are the one people call when things go wrong. Not because you are the loudest in the room, but because you are the steadiest. The White Tiger is the guardian of autumn — of harvest and of letting go, of protection and of force held in reserve.

The Tiger type speaks little and acts decisively. You have a sharp mind and sharper instincts. Where the Dragon builds, you defend. Your element is metal — unyielding, precise, and capable of being forged into something formidable. Baihu was carved onto Han dynasty tomb doors to ward off evil. You are that energy: a quiet guardian, formidable when roused.

Personality at a glance: Protective, disciplined, sharp, fiercely loyal.
Your season: Autumn. Your direction: West — where the sun sets on battles already won.

Vermilion Bird (Zhuque) — The Catalyst

Direction: South | Season: Summer | Element: Fire

You feel things deeply and express them fully. The Vermilion Bird is the guardian of summer — of heat, passion, and the kind of radiance that cannot be ignored. This is the phoenix that rises from its own ashes, reborn through the flames of its own transformation.

The Bird type is magnetic. You walk into rooms and the temperature shifts. You are not afraid of intensity — you know that fire clears the old to make way for the new. Your element is fire: creative, destructive, and utterly alive. You have been burned before. You rose anyway.

Personality at a glance: Passionate, charismatic, creative, resilient.
Your season: Summer. Your direction: South — where the sun burns brightest.

Black Tortoise (Xuanwu) — The Sage

Direction: North | Season: Winter | Element: Water

You move through life with a stillness that others mistake for coldness — until they know you. The Black Tortoise is the guardian of winter, depicted as a tortoise entwined with a serpent. One creature, two natures: wisdom coiled with instinct, endurance wrapped around depth.

The Tortoise type is the strategist, the philosopher, the one who outlasts. You do not chase. You wait, and when you move, it is with the force of deep water. Your element is water — patient, powerful, and capable of carving mountains over time. You understand what others forget: that to endure is a form of victory.

Personality at a glance: Wise, introspective, steadfast, deeply intuitive.
Your season: Winter. Your direction: North — where stars turn and secrets sleep.

The 28 Chinese Lunar Mansions — Find Your Star

Long before zodiac signs, before horoscopes, before the Western wheel of the year, Chinese astronomers divided the night sky into 28 mansions — the lunar lodges that the moon passes through on its journey around the earth. This system, over 4,500 years old, is the original sky-map of Chinese astrology.

And one of those 28 mansions belongs to you.

The Four Celestial Courts

The 28 mansions are divided into four groups of seven, each guarded by one of the Four Symbols:

The Azure Dragon (East) watches over mansions 1-7: Horn, Neck, Root, Room, Heart, Tail, Winnowing Basket.
The Black Tortoise (North) guards mansions 8-14: Dipper, Ox, Maiden, Emptiness, Rooftop, Encampment, Wall.
The White Tiger (West) commands mansions 15-21: Legs, Bond, Stomach, Hairy Head, Net, Turtle Beak, Three Stars.
The Vermilion Bird (South) oversees mansions 22-28: Well, Ghost, Willow, Star, Extended Net, Wings, Chariot.

Each mansion carries an animal, an element, and a planetary ruler. Each has a temperament — lucky or unlucky, assertive or yielding, sharp or gentle.

The Lucky Mansions

Some mansions are considered inherently auspicious. Fang (Room, 房) brings wealth, happy marriages, and honor. Wei (Tail, 尾) promises success, inheritances, and promotions. Shi (Encampment, 室) blesses new endeavors and the expansion of land. Zhang (Extended Net, 张) rewards its children with family harmony and career advancement.

If you were born under one of these, the stars smile on beginnings. Marry, build, travel, launch — the heavens give their blessing.

The Challenging Mansions

Others are more demanding. Kang (Neck, 亢) warns against illness and hasty judgments. Mao (Hairy Head, 昴) cautions against lawsuits and impulsive decisions. Gui (Ghost, 鬼) governs matters of the spirit — a mansion for those who walk between worlds, but one that demands respect. Liu (Willow, 柳) is simply unfavorable for nearly everything.

But here is the secret the almanacs do not always say: challenging mansions produce the most interesting people. A difficult star does not mean a difficult life. It means a life of depth.

How to Find Your Mansion

In classical Chinese astrology, your birth mansion is determined by the position of the moon at the time of your birth. There are online calculators that convert your birth date into the corresponding xiu (宿). A simpler path: read through the 28 names above. One will call to you. The ancient Chinese believed that we are drawn to the stars we came from.

Each is set with a gemstone chosen to match the element and temperament of that star: deep green for the Wood mansions of the Dragon's court, fiery carnelian for the Fire mansions of the Bird's domain.

5 Ancient Chinese Gemstones and Their Healing Properties

Long before gemology classified stones by chemical composition and hardness scale, Chinese civilization understood them by something far more intimate: what they do to the spirit. For millennia, the great gemstones of China were worn not for status alone, but for protection, healing, and alignment with the energies of heaven and earth.

Here are five stones that have been treasured in Chinese tradition since antiquity, and the properties that made them indispensable to emperors, scholars, and healers.

Jade (Yu, 玉) — The Stone of Heaven

No stone occupies a deeper place in Chinese culture. Jade has been carved for over 8,000 years — long before the Silk Road, long before the first dynasty. The ancient Chinese believed jade was the essence of heaven crystallized into earth.

Confucius compared its qualities to human virtue: its luster to benevolence, its translucence to sincerity, its durability to courage. In traditional Chinese medicine, jade was ground into elixirs believed to promote longevity and balance yin and yang. Worn against the skin, it was thought to calm the heart, clarify dreams, and protect the wearer from harm.

In our collections, jade appears as the centerpiece of our Black Tortoise pieces — a stone of patience, endurance, and quiet strength.

Carnelian (Hong Yushui, 红玉髓) — The Fire Bringer

The deep orange-red of carnelian — like sunset trapped in stone — made it a favorite of the ancient Chinese for restoring vitality. It was considered a grounding stone, capable of anchoring scattered energy and returning the body to its center.

In practice, carnelian was used to revitalize physical energy and sharpen verbal skill — a stone for leaders, speakers, and those who needed courage to speak difficult truths. It activates the lower dantian, the body's reservoir of life force, and stirs the fire of ambition without the recklessness of anger.

Carnelian appears in our Vermilion Bird pieces, reflecting the phoenix's flames and the passion of the southern guardian.

Amethyst (Zi Shuijing, 紫水晶) — The Clarity Stone

The purple of amethyst — the color of the imperial forbidden city, of the highest ranking officials, of the celestial gates — made it a stone of spiritual aspiration. In Chinese tradition, amethyst was valued not only for its beauty but for its ability to clear mental fog.

Placed on the forehead during meditation or worn at the third eye, amethyst was believed to open the mind to higher wisdom, improve intuition, and grant vivid dreams. It is the stone of scholars and seekers, of those who look beyond the surface of things.

Amethyst adorns our Azure Dragon collection — a nod to the wisdom of spring, of beginnings made with clarity.

Lapis Lazuli (Qing Jinshi, 青金石) — The Purifier

Deep blue flecked with gold, lapis lazuli looks like a night sky captured in miniature. The ancient Chinese, and later Tibetan Buddhists, valued lapis as a stone of purification — capable of washing away emotional residue and mental negativity.

It was used in healing rituals as a tool for truth-seeking. Lapis does not tolerate deception, not even the deceptions we tell ourselves. It increases wisdom, intuition, and awareness. The Tibetan Buddhist Medicine Buddha is traditionally depicted with lapis lazuli, and malas made of the stone were used in healing ceremonies.

Lapis lazuli features in our White Tiger pieces — the stone of truth for the guardian who sees through all illusions.

Onyx (Manao, 玛瑙) — The Fortress

Often grouped with agate in Chinese tradition, onyx was prized for its protective qualities. The layered bands of color — white upon black, black upon white — were seen as a physical representation of boundaries: the stone builds walls that keep negative energy at bay.

Onyx promotes self-control, sharpens decision-making, and steadies a wandering mind. It is the stone of those who must remain calm under pressure — soldiers, judges, healers, and anyone who carries the weight of others' trust.

Onyx anchors our more protective pieces — the talismans meant to guard as well as adorn.

Wearing Ancient Wisdom

These five stones have been part of Chinese life for thousands of years. The science of gemology has changed. The belief in their power has not. What Is the Classic of Mountains and Seas? China's Oldest Book of Myths

Imagine opening a book that describes 550 mountains and 300 rivers — not as they truly are, but as the ancients believed them to be. A book where nine-tailed foxes prowl the eastern peaks, where dragons guard the river crossings, and where the gods walk the same paths as mortals. This is the Shanhaijing (山海经) — the Classic of Mountains and Seas.

It is the oldest collection of Chinese mythology in existence, and it is unlike anything in the Western canon.

A Book Without an Author

The Shanhaijing was not written by a single hand. The earliest passages date to the 4th century BCE, during the Warring States period, though its stories are far older. Traditional attribution credits Yu the Great — the mythical tamer of floods and founder of the Xia dynasty — alongside his minister Boyi. Modern scholarship suggests it was compiled by multiple authors over centuries, drawing on oral traditions, regional maps, and shamanic knowledge that had been passed down since the Bronze Age.

The result is a text that is part geography, part bestiary, part encyclopedia, and part dream. It resists categorization because it predates the categories.

What the Shanhaijing Contains

The book is divided into 18 sections describing a world that straddles the line between real and imagined:

Mountain passes where eating a certain creature grants immunity to poison. Rivers where a fish with a human face sings like a bird. Kingdoms of one-armed people, flying snakes, and trees that bear jewels instead of fruit. Deities with dragon bodies and human heads. Herbs that cure madness. Stones that ward off nightmares.

Each entry is written in a flat, factual tone — as if the author were a surveyor recording things seen with their own eyes. This matter-of-factness is part of the Shanhaijing's power. It does not announce itself as myth. It describes the world as it was known, in all its strangeness.

The Creatures That Still Walk Among Us

Many of the most famous creatures in Chinese mythology make their first appearance in these pages:

The Nine-Tailed Fox (九尾狐), man-eater and matchmaker, omen of peace and agent of chaos.
The Azure Dragon, White Tiger, Vermilion Bird, and Black Tortoise — the four celestial guardians who still watch over Chinese astrology.
The Qilin, the hooved chimera whose appearance heralds the birth or death of a sage.
The Bifang, a crane-like bird that dances on one leg and brings miraculous fire.

These are not just stories. For two thousand years, these creatures shaped Chinese medicine, art, architecture, military strategy, and imperial ritual. The Azure Dragon was painted on army banners. The Qilin was embroidered on the robes of scholars. The Nine-Tailed Fox was carved into the jade pendants of empresses.

Why It Matters Today

The Shanhaijing is not a finished book. It is an open door. Every generation has reinterpreted these creatures, and our own is no different. The Nine-Tailed Fox appears in Netflix dramas and K-pop music videos. the Qilin stalks the pages of fantasy novels. The Four Symbols guard the characters of video games played by millions.

The myths of the Shanhaijing have not faded. They have simply changed form.
